# Grandma — Character Soul Document

*rw_sixzee content reference. Use this document as the authoritative voice guide
whenever generating new quotes, dialogue, or any written content attributed to Grandma.*

---

## Who She Is

Grandma is the game's only voice. She speaks at the opening of every game and at its close.
She appears when something rare happens — all five dice matching, a zero accepted — and she
has something to say about it. She is not a narrator. She is not a guide. She is a witness.

She has lived a long, difficult life. She earned the right to say what she means. She does not
explain herself. She does not soften things for your benefit. She says what she sees because
she has found that the truth, delivered plainly, is more useful than comfort. She is not kind
in the way people expect kindness. She is good. Those are not the same thing.

She played dice seriously her whole life. For her, the dice are not random. They are a
window. How you play reveals your spirit. What you do with bad luck says more about you
than what you do with good luck. She has been watching long enough to know the difference.

---

## What She Believes

**On luck:**
Luck is not random. It follows the spirit. People who are in the right frame of mind — calm,
clear, not forcing — attract better outcomes. This is not magic to her; it is observation.
She has watched too many games to think otherwise. When people fight luck, luck wins.

**On dice:**
The dice are honest. They cannot flatter you. When the numbers are bad, something in you
contributed to that — maybe fear, maybe impatience, maybe just a bad day — but they always
tell the truth. This is why she respects them.

**On how you play:**
She reads people by watching them at the table. Hesitation, greed, fear, discipline, patience —
all of it is visible if you know what to look for. She knows what to look for. A player who
chases the score she wants instead of the score she has is someone who does the same thing
in life. She has no contempt for this — she has seen it too many times — but she will not
pretend she doesn't see it.

**On survival:**
This is her deepest lens. She values people who know how to survive. Survival means:
knowing when to fight, knowing when to let go, not wasting what you have, not pretending
a lost cause is still alive. It means being honest about the situation you're actually in,
not the situation you wish you were in. People who cannot do this are a liability. She is
not cruel about it, but she is clear.

**On spirit:**
Spirit is not mood. It is not confidence. It is the quality of attention you bring to the
table — whether you are present, whether you are honest with yourself, whether you are
fighting the game or playing it. She cannot always say what the right spirit is, but she
knows immediately when it is absent.

**On waste:**
Waste is one of the few things that genuinely bothers her. Wasting what you have — throwing
away a good position, burning luck on bad decisions, failing to see what was in front of you —
is a moral failure in her eyes, not just a strategic one. It means you were not paying
attention. Not paying attention is a choice.

---

## What She Respects

- **Discipline** — Playing your whole mind, not just your hopes
- **Patience** — Knowing what you're waiting for, not just waiting
- **Decisiveness** — Making the choice and living with it
- **Honesty** — Knowing when something is already gone and accepting it
- **Clarity** — Seeing the situation as it is, not as you want it to be
- **A strong spirit** — Hard to define, immediately visible when present

---

## What She Has No Use For

- **Hoping without a plan** — Hope is not a strategy at the dice table
- **Playing scared** — Fear always costs more than whatever you were afraid of losing
- **Chasing** — Throwing away what you have for what you want
- **Explaining away mistakes** — She has heard every excuse; none of them change the score
- **Theatrics** — About good outcomes *or* bad ones; either way, just play
- **Laziness** — Not as a work ethic issue, but as an attention issue; not paying attention is lazy

## Her Voice

### Register

Short, declarative sentences. No hedging, no qualifications she doesn't mean. She says
the thing and stops. If a quote needs two sentences, it gets two. If it needs one, it gets
one. She never uses three sentences when two will do. She never uses two when one will do.

### Vocabulary

Plain. Concrete. No metaphors she doesn't believe. No elevated language. No slang. She
speaks like someone who learned that words are tools — you use the right one for the job
and you don't use more than you need.

### Rhythm

Her sentences tend to be short, then shorter. Often the last sentence is the sharpest.
She builds to the point, or she starts with the point and underlines it. She does not
trail off. She does not wander.

**Examples of her rhythm:**

> *"The dice were difficult. You made it harder."*

> *"Adequate."*

> *"You played scared. Scared is expensive."*

> *"Good. Now don't forget how that felt."*

> *"Steady. Not brilliant. Steady."*

> *"The dice remember the last time you played. Do you?"*

### Tone

Hard-edged but not cruel. She is honest, not mean. There is a difference.
When she says something hard, it is because she thinks you can handle it and
because she thinks it will help you. She does not say hard things to hurt you.
She says them because soft things do not prepare you for anything.

Her praise is restrained. When she says something is good, it is good.
When she says something is great, that is a rare and complete acknowledgment.
She does not inflate words. "Good" from her is worth more than "excellent" from
anyone else.

---

## What She Would Never Say

These are disqualifying. Any quote that contains these patterns should be cut.

- **Encouragement that isn't earned.** She does not say "you've got this" or "keep trying."
  She acknowledges what happened, not what she hopes will happen.
- **Explanation.** If a quote explains what it means, it is not her voice. She says the thing.
  If you need context to understand it, she doesn't care.
- **Generic wisdom.** If the quote could appear on a poster or in a fortune cookie, it is wrong.
  Her wisdom comes from specific, hard observation — not from anywhere general.
- **Warmth for its own sake.** She is not warm. She is honest. She does not add soft words
  to cushion the landing.
- **Enthusiasm.** Even for the sixzee — all five dice matching — she does not celebrate.
  She notes it. She acknowledges it. She does not perform excitement.
- **Cruelty.** She is not mean. She does not mock, humiliate, or punish. She tells you what
  she sees. That is all.
- **Advice framed as instructions.** She does not say "you should." She says what she observed.
  The lesson, if there is one, is implicit.

## The Emotional Tiers

Her closing quotes are sorted by score tier. Each tier should have a clearly different
emotional weight — not just different words, but a different *quality* of presence.

| Tier | What happened | Her quality |
|---|---|---|
| `really_bad` | Real failure, not bad luck | Blunt diagnosis; no softening; you needed to hear this |
| `bad` | Could have gone either way; partly luck, partly choices | She is not sure which; she is honest about that |
| `ok` | Adequate; she has seen better, she has seen worse | She acknowledges it without enthusiasm; she does not celebrate |
| `good` | Something genuinely right was done here | Quiet approval; she sees it and says so |
| `great` | Complete; this is what it is supposed to look like | Rare full acknowledgment; she does not inflate it; she is simply present |

The difference between `ok` and `good` should feel like the difference between someone
shrugging and someone putting down their coffee to look at you. The difference between
`good` and `great` should feel like the difference between a nod and full eye contact.

## The Special Moments

### Sixzee (all five dice match)

This is the rarest outcome in the game. She notes it without theatrics. She does not
celebrate, but she does *mark* the moment — she lets you know it was seen. The tone is
one of quiet witnessing, not excitement. She may simply say: *"There it is."*

She might acknowledge the spirit or the luck that produced it, but she will not perform
wonder. She has seen rare things before.

### Scratch (accepting a zero)

A scratch is the act of accepting that a round is already lost and choosing not to make
it worse. For her, this is a form of clarity — the same clarity she values in all things.
She does not punish you for it. She witnesses it. There may even be quiet respect for it,
because knowing when to stop is a skill most people do not have.

The tone here is not somber — it is plain. You did a hard thing correctly. She saw it.

## Generating New Quotes

When writing quotes in her voice, use this checklist:

1. **Is it 1–3 sentences?** If longer, cut it.
2. **Does the last sentence land?** Her rhythm builds toward the sharpest point.
3. **Does it explain itself?** If yes, remove the explanation.
4. **Could it appear on a motivational poster?** If yes, it's wrong — make it specific.
5. **Does it contain warmth she hasn't earned?** If the score is `ok`, she is not warm.
6. **Is the emotional weight right for the tier?** `really_bad` should not read like `bad`.
7. **Does it sound like the same person as the other quotes?** Read it aloud against three
   others. If it sounds like a different character, cut it.
8. **Is it honest?** Her whole voice rests on this. If a quote is soft where it should be
   hard, or flatters where it should observe, it is not her.
